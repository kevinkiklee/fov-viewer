85mm vs 200mm — Field of View Comparison

Portrait to telephoto — 200mm captures less than half the width of 85mm.

Placing 85mm and 200mm side by side reveals one of the most striking comparisons in this set. On a full-frame sensor, 85mm covers 28.6° horizontally while 200mm covers just 12.4° — meaning the 85mm frame is more than twice as wide. At 200mm you are looking at a narrow, isolated slice of a scene, which is exactly what sports, wildlife, and outdoor portrait photographers need when their subjects are distant or when a clean, compressed background is essential to the image.

The concept of "lens compression" is often misunderstood, and this comparison is a useful way to think about it clearly. Compression is not a property of the lens itself — it is a consequence of the distance from which the photograph is taken. To fill the same portion of the frame with a subject at 200mm, the photographer must stand much further back than at 85mm. From that greater distance, both the subject and the background are proportionally more distant from the camera, making background elements appear larger relative to the subject. The 200mm lens does not compress the scene; it simply requires a shooting position from which the scene naturally appears more compressed.

For practical shooting, 200mm also benefits significantly from image stabilisation (IS or VR). At such a long focal length, even slight camera movement can blur a shot, and most modern 70-200mm and 100-400mm zooms include optical stabilisation as a result. The 85mm, by contrast, is typically hand-holdable without stabilisation in good light. For outdoor portrait sessions where subjects can move freely around a large garden or field, 200mm allows the photographer to remain unobtrusive at 8–10 metres and still fill the frame with a single subject — a quality shared with wildlife and sports telephoto work that makes the focal length genuinely multi-purpose beyond its obvious reach advantage.
