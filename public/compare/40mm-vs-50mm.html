40mm vs 50mm — Field of View Comparison

Two normal lenses with more difference than most photographers expect.

On paper, 40mm and 50mm look almost identical — they are separated by just 10mm and are both classified as "normal" focal lengths. In practice, their 10° field of view difference (56.8° vs 46.8° on a full-frame sensor) is wider than most photographers anticipate before trying both side by side. That 10° difference means the 40mm frame captures roughly 21% more scene width, which is noticeable when photographing people indoors or in tight urban environments where a few centimetres of extra working distance are the difference between a good shot and a scrambled repositioning.

The Canon EF 40mm f/2.8 STM pancake lens did a great deal to revive interest in the 40mm focal length. Launched in 2012, it was so compact that it changed the feel of a full-frame DSLR entirely, and its optical quality easily exceeded expectations for its price. That lens introduced many photographers to a perspective they had never considered, and a significant number of them found that 40mm suited their shooting style better than the 50mm they had previously considered the natural starting point.

While 40mm offers more environmental context, 50mm remains the king of shallow depth-of-field isolation among normal lenses. The slightly longer focal length allows a faster aperture to produce more background blur at a given subject distance, and the more compressed rendering keeps subjects looking proportional. For portrait photographers who want a natural look without a dedicated 85mm, 50mm at f/1.4 or f/1.8 is a proven choice. For street photographers who want to travel light and stay unobtrusive, 40mm provides just enough extra width to capture candid moments without stepping back.
