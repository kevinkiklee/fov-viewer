Full Frame vs Micro Four Thirds at 50mm — Field of View Comparison

The 2.0× crop factor doubles the effective focal length — a dramatic reach advantage.

The Micro Four Thirds (M4/3) sensor standard, developed jointly by Olympus and Panasonic, uses a sensor with a 2.0× crop factor relative to full frame. Mounting a 50mm lens on an M4/3 body such as the Olympus OM-D E-M1 or Panasonic Lumix G series produces a field of view equivalent to a 100mm lens on full frame — approximately 24.0° horizontally, compared to 46.8° on full frame. That is almost a halving of the visible scene width from the same shooting position.

The reach advantage this provides for telephoto shooting is remarkable. A 300mm lens on an M4/3 body is equivalent to a 600mm lens on full frame — a reach that would require a very expensive, very heavy supertelephoto lens on a larger sensor system. This is why wildlife and bird photographers are among the most enthusiastic adopters of M4/3 systems: Olympus's 300mm f/4 IS Pro and Panasonic's 100-400mm zoom give 600mm and 800mm equivalent reach in packages that weigh a fraction of their full-frame counterparts. The system also enables genuinely hand-holdable telephoto shooting thanks to powerful in-body stabilisation in flagship bodies.

The trade-off is equally significant at the wide end. Because every focal length is effectively doubled, achieving the perspective of a 24mm wide-angle on full frame requires a 12mm lens on M4/3. Going wider than that — equivalent to 20mm or 14mm on full frame — requires specialty ultra-wide lenses. The M4/3 system has good options in this range (such as the Olympus 7-14mm f/2.8 and Panasonic 8-18mm f/2.8-4), but they are more expensive relative to the equivalent full-frame lenses than mid-telephoto options are. For travel photographers who mostly shoot landscapes and cityscapes rather than wildlife, this is a meaningful consideration when choosing a system.
