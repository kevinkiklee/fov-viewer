24mm vs 35mm — Field of View Comparison

Two popular wide-angle primes — more inclusion versus more naturalism.

The 24mm and 35mm focal lengths are two of the most commonly recommended first primes, yet they serve meaningfully different creative purposes. On a full-frame sensor, 24mm delivers a 84.1° horizontal field of view, making it an excellent choice for architecture, environmental photography, and any situation where you need to capture a broad scene in a tight space. The wider angle lets you include foreground elements that anchor a composition while still revealing the full context of a scene.

35mm narrows the view to 63.4°, a difference of 20.7° that translates into noticeably less peripheral distortion and a more natural-looking perspective. At 35mm, straight lines near the edges of the frame stay closer to their real-world appearance, which is why the focal length is a favourite for documentary work, street photography, and environmental portraits. Subjects look like people rather than slightly caricatured by the wider projection of 24mm.

The trade-off between these two lenses is fundamentally a question of inclusion versus naturalism. When you need to fit a group of people inside a small room, or when you want to show exactly where a subject stands in relation to a vast landscape, 24mm is the right tool. When you want the viewer to feel like they are standing next to the photographer, sharing the same intimate perspective, 35mm is usually the better choice. Many photographers who own both reach for 35mm as their day-to-day lens and reserve 24mm for situations that genuinely demand the extra width.
