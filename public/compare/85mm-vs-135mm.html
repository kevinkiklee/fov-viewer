85mm vs 135mm — Field of View Comparison

Both portrait lenses — but different working distances and background compression.

The 85mm and 135mm focal lengths are often spoken of in the same breath as portrait lenses, but they produce quite different results in practice. On a full-frame sensor, 85mm delivers a 28.6° horizontal field of view and is typically used at a subject distance of roughly 2 metres for a flattering head-and-shoulders portrait. The 85mm is compact enough to be unobtrusive indoors, fast enough in its f/1.4 and f/1.8 variants to separate subjects beautifully from busy backgrounds, and wide enough to capture environmental context when needed.

135mm narrows the view further to 18.2°, a 10.4° reduction that feels significant when framing a person. To achieve a comparable head-and-shoulders composition at 135mm, the photographer typically stands approximately 3.5 metres away. That additional distance produces two effects worth understanding separately: the background elements appear larger relative to the subject (because the photographer is standing further back), and the facial features render with even less perspective distortion, creating the characteristic "flat" look that editorial and fashion photographers prize. The 135mm is also less likely to feel intrusive to subjects at events or in documentary situations — the extra distance creates a quiet, unobserved quality to many 135mm portraits.

Both lenses are capable of exceptional bokeh, but the nature of the blur differs. At equivalent apertures and subject sizes, 135mm produces more background blur due to the greater subject-to-background distance required by the longer focal length. The 85mm, however, is far more practical in confined spaces — a small studio, a wedding venue, or a narrow street — where backing up to 3.5 metres is simply not possible. Many portrait photographers who own both treat 85mm as their indoor lens and reach for 135mm when they have the room to work.
