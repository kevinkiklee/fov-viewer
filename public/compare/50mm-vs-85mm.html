50mm vs 85mm — Field of View Comparison

Two of the most popular primes — versatile standard vs portrait workhorse.

No two prime lenses are more frequently recommended for beginners than the 50mm and the 85mm. They represent adjacent rungs on the focal length ladder, yet they serve quite different photographic purposes. On a full-frame sensor, 50mm yields a 46.8° horizontal field of view — close enough to natural human vision to feel familiar and unforced. Its versatility is legendary: it works for street, documentary, portraits, food, travel, and countless other genres without ever feeling like the wrong tool. The 50mm is the lens many photographers keep on their camera as a default.

85mm narrows the view sharply to 28.6°, an 18° difference that is dramatic when framing a person. Where 50mm at arm's length might show a subject from the waist up along with the background behind them, 85mm at the same distance fills more of the frame with face and shoulders and throws the background further into blur. More importantly, the longer focal length encourages — and practically requires — the photographer to stand further back, typically 2–3 metres for a flattering head-and-shoulders framing. That extra distance means facial features are rendered with less perspective distortion: noses appear more proportional, ears remain visible, and the overall impression is more flattering.

The 18° difference between these two focal lengths is one of the most meaningful steps in the standard prime progression. Switching from 50mm to 85mm for a portrait session is not merely a zoom substitute — it changes the relationship between photographer and subject, the character of the background blur, and the rendering of facial geometry. Both lenses deserve a place in a working photographer's kit, and for anyone building a two-prime system, 50mm + 85mm is one of the most time-tested combinations in the history of the medium.
