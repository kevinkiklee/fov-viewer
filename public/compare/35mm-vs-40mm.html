35mm vs 40mm — Field of View Comparison

A subtle but perceptible difference — 40mm is the underappreciated sweet spot.

The 6.6° gap between 35mm and 40mm is the smallest comparison on this site, yet it is one of the most instructive. On a full-frame sensor, 35mm produces a 63.4° horizontal field of view and 40mm produces 56.8°. Side by side in the tool, the difference appears modest, but in practice it has real optical and compositional consequences. The 40mm focal length sits in a zone that many optical designers consider ideal for minimising both barrel distortion (common below 35mm) and the compressed perspective that starts to become noticeable above 50mm.

The 40mm focal length has historically been overlooked because it falls between the two most marketed primes — 35mm and 50mm. Yet several camera makers have gravitated toward it for good reason. The Voigtlander Nokton 40mm f/1.2 is prized by Leica users precisely for its balance of width, rendering, and compact size. The Ricoh GR III uses a 18.3mm lens on its APS-C sensor, which gives a 40mm full-frame equivalent field of view — and that camera has developed a devoted following among street photographers who value exactly the perspective that focal length provides.

Choosing between 35mm and 40mm often comes down to how you approach close subjects. At 35mm you can fill a frame with a person's upper body while still showing some of the room behind them; at 40mm the framing tightens just enough to bring the subject forward visually, with noticeably less stretching of facial features near the edges. If you already own a 35mm and wonder whether 40mm would feel different enough to justify a second lens, the answer is yes — not dramatically, but consistently and in a way that becomes clear over time.
