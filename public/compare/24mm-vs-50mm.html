24mm vs 50mm — Field of View Comparison

Wide angle vs standard — nearly double the viewing angle separates these two lenses.

The 37° gap between 24mm and 50mm is one of the largest in the normal-to-wide-angle range. On a full-frame sensor, 24mm covers 84.1° horizontally while 50mm covers 46.8° — meaning 24mm captures nearly twice the angular width. Placing them side by side in the viewer makes this difference viscerally clear in a way that numbers alone rarely convey. A scene that requires you to step all the way back against a wall at 50mm can be comfortably framed at 24mm from the middle of a room.

This pairing also explains why the 24-70mm zoom range became the default "workhorse" focal length for professional photojournalists and event photographers. At 24mm you can capture a full banquet table, an altar and the congregation behind it, or a mountain range with foreground wildflowers. At 50mm from the same position you are focused on one half of the table, the ceremony couple, or a single peak. The zoom does not just change magnification — it fundamentally changes what story the photograph tells about the world it depicts.

On an APS-C sensor with a 1.5× crop factor, 24mm becomes approximately 36mm equivalent and 50mm becomes approximately 75mm equivalent. This is one of the reasons APS-C photographers sometimes find the 24mm more useful than on full frame — the slightly longer effective focal length reduces the extreme distortion of corners that can plague 24mm on larger sensors. For the same reason, a 50mm on APS-C behaves more like a short portrait lens than a standard normal, subtly reshaping how each focal length fits into a shooting style.
